## 3.1 The four-stage LLM lifecycle, narrated

When someone says "we built an LLM," they're usually compressing a four-stage pipeline that takes months and millions of dollars from raw text to deployed product. Let me walk through it.

**Stage 1 — Pretraining**: You take a transformer (decoder-only, billions of parameters), feed it 1-15 trillion tokens of text scraped from the web plus books plus code, and train it for weeks on thousands of GPUs to predict the next token. The result is a "base model" that can complete text but doesn't know how to follow instructions, refuse harmful requests, or chat. It's a savant that has read most of the internet.

**Stage 2 — Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)**: You collect 100K to 10M (instruction, response) pairs — written by humans or generated by other LLMs — and continue training the base model on these. The loss is the same cross-entropy on next-token, but masked so we only train on the response side. After SFT, the model knows how to follow instructions in a chat format. It can now act as an assistant.

**Stage 3 — Alignment**: SFT teaches "what a good response looks like." Alignment teaches "what response humans actually prefer." We collect preference data — pairs where humans rate one response as better than another — and tune the model to prefer the chosen one. Originally done with RLHF (reward model + PPO); now usually with DPO (direct preference optimization, simpler and more stable). After alignment, the model is helpful, mostly harmless, and reasonably honest.

**Stage 4 — Inference / Deployment**: The aligned model is quantized (FP8/INT8/INT4), loaded into a serving framework like vLLM or TensorRT-LLM, exposed via an API, monitored with metrics (TTFT, TPOT, error rate), and continuously evaluated against drift. This is where AI Engineering — your day job — happens.

### The block diagram

```
┌─────────────────┐   ┌─────────────────┐   ┌─────────────────┐   ┌─────────────────┐
│ 1. Pretraining  │   │ 2. SFT          │   │ 3. Alignment    │   │ 4. Deployment   │
│ next-token on   │──▶│ instruction     │──▶│ RLHF / DPO      │──▶│ vLLM,           │
│ 1-15T tokens    │   │ following       │   │ preference      │   │ continuous      │
│                 │   │ 100K-10M pairs  │   │ optimization    │   │ batching,       │
│                 │   │                 │   │                 │   │ KV-cache,       │
│                 │   │                 │   │                 │   │ monitoring      │
└─────────────────┘   └─────────────────┘   └─────────────────┘   └─────────────────┘
   weeks-months           hours-days            hours-days           ongoing
   $millions              $thousands            $thousands           $$ per token
   ↓                      ↓                     ↓                    ↓
   Base model             Instruct model        Aligned model        Production
   (savant)               (follows orders)      (preferred answers)  (your job)
```

Base = foundation. SFT = shapes format. Alignment = shapes preferences. Deployment = scales it.

### How to say this in an interview

> "Modern LLM development is a four-stage pipeline. Pretraining is the giant compute spend — train a transformer on trillions of tokens with next-token prediction, get a base model that has absorbed broad knowledge but doesn't follow instructions. SFT adds instruction-following by fine-tuning on curated (instruction, response) pairs. Alignment — usually DPO now, formerly RLHF — tunes for human preference using paired choice data. Deployment uses vLLM or TensorRT-LLM with quantization, paged attention, continuous batching, prefix caching. Each stage has different compute footprints — pretraining is millions of dollars, SFT and alignment are thousands. As an AI engineer, my job is mostly stage four, with occasional fine-tuning at stage two and rarely participating in pretraining."

## 3.2 Stage 0 — Tokenization

Before any of this, raw text has to become numbers. That's tokenization. It seems trivial until you discover that a poorly-chosen tokenizer can double your inference cost on Arabic.

### Why not characters or whole words?

**Characters**: Sequences too long. "Hello, world!" is 13 characters but 3-4 tokens with BPE. With character-level, your transformer has to look 4x further to span the same semantic unit. Compute is quadratic in sequence length; this is brutal.

**Whole words**: 100K-1M vocab. Out-of-vocabulary explosion (every typo, brand name, hashtag is unknown). Multilingual nightmare.

**Subword (BPE, WordPiece, Unigram)**: The sweet spot. Common words become single tokens; rare words split into known pieces. Vocabulary stays manageable (32K-256K). No OOV (with byte-level fallback).

### Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE) — walked through

The algorithm:

1. Start with character-level vocabulary.
2. Find the most frequent adjacent pair in the corpus. Merge it into a new token.
3. Repeat until vocab size hits target (e.g., 50K or 128K).

Let's walk through a tiny example. Suppose the training corpus is:

```
"low low low low low lowest newer newer newer newer newer newer wider"
```

After splitting to characters with end-of-word markers:

```
l o w </w>     (×5)
l o w e s t </w>  (×1)
n e w e r </w>  (×6)
w i d e r </w>  (×1)
```

Most frequent adjacent pair: `e r` (count 7 from "newer" ×6 + "wider" ×1). Merge:

```
l o w </w>     (×5)
l o w e s t </w>
n e w er </w>  (×6)
w i d er </w>
```

Next most frequent: `n e` (×6). Merge:

```
l o w </w>     (×5)
l o w e s t </w>
ne w er </w>  (×6)
w i d er </w>
```

Continue merging until vocab fills. After many steps you might have tokens like `low`, `er`, `est`, `ne`, `w`, etc. A new word like `lower` would tokenize as `low` + `er` — two tokens, both known.

### Byte-level BPE — what GPT-2/3/4 use

The catch with character-level BPE: you still need a vocab of all possible characters. For Unicode that's 100K+ symbols, and you'd miss exotic emoji or rare scripts.

Byte-level BPE operates on raw UTF-8 bytes. There are exactly 256 possible bytes — every text on earth representable. No OOV ever. Multilingual, code, emoji, all handled uniformly. GPT-2 introduced this; tiktoken (OpenAI's tokenizer library) uses it; LLaMA-3 switched to it.

The cost: bytes don't align with characters in non-ASCII languages, so a single Chinese character can be 3 bytes = often 3 tokens. Arabic, Korean, Japanese all suffer. LLaMA-3 mitigated this by expanding vocab to 128K and including more whole-script tokens.

### SentencePiece — the framework

SentencePiece (Google) is a *framework* (not a single algorithm). It treats input as a raw Unicode stream — no whitespace pre-tokenization, no language assumptions. Under the hood you can pick BPE or Unigram. SentencePiece is the standard for multilingual models (mT5, NLLB, Gemma).

The killer feature: round-trip safety. Tokenize then detokenize, you get back exactly your input. With BPE that requires careful handling of leading-space tokens (`▁hello` where `▁` represents a space). SentencePiece handles this natively.

### WordPiece (BERT)

Same as BPE but the merge criterion is **likelihood**, not raw frequency. Pick the merge that maximizes the training corpus likelihood under the resulting vocab. Slightly different vocab from BPE; in practice indistinguishable performance.

### The tokenization comparison table

| Tokenizer | Algorithm | Vocab | Used in |
|-----------|-----------|-------|---------|
| **BPE** | Frequency merges | 32K-256K | GPT-2, LLaMA-1/2 |
| **Byte-level BPE** | BPE on bytes | 50K-128K | GPT-3/4 (tiktoken), LLaMA-3 |
| **WordPiece** | Likelihood merges | 30K | BERT, DistilBERT |
| **SentencePiece (Unigram)** | EM over candidate vocab | 32K-256K | T5, mT5, Gemma |
| **SentencePiece (BPE)** | BPE on Unicode | 32K-256K | LLaMA-2, Mistral, Qwen |

```
   │   Pre-tokenizer     │   (whitespace, punctuation)
   │   (optional)        │
   └──────────┬──────────┘
              │
              ▼

```

### Token economics

- English: ~1 token per 0.75 words, or 1.3 tokens per word.
- Code: ~1 token per 0.5 words (lots of punctuation, special chars).
- CJK (Chinese, Japanese, Korean) without optimized vocab: 1-3 tokens per character.
- Arabic with non-Arabic-optimized tokenizer: 3-5x more tokens than English for the same content.

This matters economically. If you're billing per token and your tokenizer is bad for the user's language, you're charging 3-5x more for the same value. Pick the tokenizer that matches your user base.

### Common mistakes / gotchas

1. **The leading-space problem**: BPE tokenizers emit tokens like `▁hello` (with a leading space marker). If you string-match for stop sequences without accounting for this, you'll miss them. Always use the tokenizer's `decode()` to round-trip safely.
2. **Adding tokens at inference without retraining the embedding**: If you add new special tokens (e.g., a custom `<|tool_call|>`), the embedding for that token is random — model has never seen it. You need a fine-tune to teach the model what it means.
3. **Counting tokens with the wrong tokenizer**: GPT-4's tokenizer differs from GPT-3's, which differs from LLaMA's. Don't count with `tiktoken.get_encoding('cl100k_base')` and bill against `gpt-4o` which uses a different encoding.

### Resume tie-in

> When I built the Claude-powered ML workspace at TrueBalance, tokenization mattered for cost. We were sending Jenkins logs and Athena query results to Claude as context, and these can be huge. By using Claude's tokenizer to count exactly, and aggressively trimming or summarizing log tails before sending, we kept the average cost-per-task at about $0.04 instead of $0.20. That's purely tokenization-aware engineering — no model change.

### Interview Q&A

**Q1. Why subword tokenization rather than words or chars?**
> "Words give you a 100K-1M vocab and OOV explosion — every typo, brand, hashtag is unknown. Characters keep vocab tiny but make sequences 4-5x longer, which kills attention's quadratic cost. Subword — BPE, WordPiece, Unigram — splits common words into single tokens and rare words into known pieces. Vocab stays around 50K-128K, no OOV with byte fallback, sequences are reasonable length. It's the empirical sweet spot every modern LLM uses."

**Q2. Walk me through BPE.**
> "Start with character-level vocabulary. Count adjacent pair frequencies across the training corpus. Merge the most frequent pair into a new token. Add it to vocab. Repeat until vocab size reaches target. So `low low lowest` first might merge `l o → lo`, then `lo w → low`, then process `lowest` similarly. New text like `lower` tokenizes by greedy longest-match using the learned merge rules — `low + er`. Byte-level BPE does this on UTF-8 bytes instead of characters, which guarantees no OOV ever and handles all scripts uniformly. That's what GPT-3/4 and LLaMA-3 use."

**Q3. SentencePiece vs BPE — what's the difference?**
> "SentencePiece is a framework, not an algorithm — under the hood it can use BPE or Unigram. The key thing it does differently from naive BPE is operate directly on Unicode without language-specific pre-tokenization. So it works the same for English, Chinese, code, emoji — no whitespace assumption. It also handles round-trip safety: tokenize then detokenize and you get the exact input back. SentencePiece is the standard for multilingual models like T5, mT5, Gemma. LLaMA-2 and Mistral also use SentencePiece-BPE."

**Q4. What's the leading-space gotcha in BPE?**
> "Most BPE tokenizers emit tokens with a leading space marker — typically `▁` (lower one-eighth block) for SentencePiece, or just `Ġ` (capital G with acute) for tiktoken. So `' hello'` and `'hello'` are different tokens. If you're matching stop sequences as strings without going through the tokenizer's decode, you'll miss them. Always use `tokenizer.decode()` for output and account for these markers in any string-matching logic."

**Q5. Why does Arabic cost 3-5x more tokens than English with a non-optimized tokenizer?**
> "Tokenizers like GPT-3's were trained on web data heavily skewed toward English. Arabic was under-represented, so the merge algorithm never learned good Arabic subwords. Result: Arabic gets tokenized at byte level — each Arabic character is 2-3 UTF-8 bytes, and each byte often becomes a token. So a 100-character Arabic sentence might be 300-500 tokens versus 60-80 for the same English content. For a UAE deployment, you pick a tokenizer trained with proper Arabic representation — Qwen, Gemma multilingual, or BGE-M3 for embeddings."

## 3.3 Stage 1 — Pretraining (next-token prediction)

The objective is the simplest possible: given the first t-1 tokens, maximize the probability of the t-th. That's it. Cross-entropy on next-token, summed over every position, every sequence, trillions of tokens.

### The math

For a sequence `x_1, x_2, ..., x_n`:

```
L = - (1/n) Σ_t log P(x_t | x_<t)
```

Plain English: at every position, the model assigns a probability to every vocabulary token; we take the negative log of the probability assigned to the actual next token; average over all positions; that's our loss.

### Block diagram of one training step

```
    Input sequence (B × T tokens):
    "The quick brown fox jumps over the lazy dog"
         │
         ▼
    ┌─────────────────────────┐
    │ Tokenizer + embedding   │
    └──────────┬──────────────┘
               │ (B × T × d_model)
               ▼
    ┌─────────────────────────┐
    │  Transformer (N layers) │
    └──────────┬──────────────┘
               │ (B × T × d_model)
               ▼
    ┌─────────────────────────┐
    │ Final LayerNorm + LM    │
    │ head (linear → vocab)   │
    └──────────┬──────────────┘
               │ (B × T × V)  logits
               ▼
    Shift right by one and compute CE:
    target[t] = input[t+1]
               │
               ▼
    ┌─────────────────────────┐
    │ CrossEntropyLoss        │
    │ averaged over all       │
    │ positions and batch     │
    └──────────┬──────────────┘
               │ scalar loss
               ▼
            backprop
               │
               ▼
            AdamW step
```

### Worked example with numbers

LLaMA-3 70B pretraining:

- Parameters: 70B
- Tokens: 15T (210 tokens per parameter — Chinchilla-optimal would be ~20)
- Compute: forward + backward = 6 × params × tokens = 6 × 70e9 × 15e12 = 6.3 × 10^24 FLOPs
- At 1.5 PFLOP per H100 sustained: 4.2 × 10^9 H100-seconds = ~50,000 H100-days
- Cluster of 16K H100s for 80 days

Cost: rough rule of thumb $2 per H100-hour, so 50,000 H100-days × 24 h × $2 ≈ $2.4M for compute alone. Plus engineers, plus data acquisition, plus failed runs — frontier pretraining is $10M+.

### Data — what goes in

- Web crawl (Common Crawl, refined): trillions of tokens, but quality varies wildly. Modern recipes use heavy filtering — perplexity filtering, duplicate detection, quality classifiers.
- Books: 100B-300B tokens. Higher quality, narrative structure.
- Code (GitHub): 200B-500B tokens. Boosts reasoning, especially math and step-by-step thinking.
- Wikipedia: ~5B tokens. High quality, encyclopedic.
- ArXiv, PubMed: scientific reasoning.
- Synthetic data: 2024-2026 trend. Use a smaller LLM to generate high-quality reasoning traces, then train on them. Phi-3 and Phi-4 went heavy on this.

### Chinchilla scaling laws — the rule of thumb

DeepMind's 2022 paper showed: for a fixed compute budget, the optimal allocation is roughly **20 tokens per parameter**. So a 70B model wants 1.4T tokens, not 200B. Most pre-Chinchilla models were severely under-trained.

Modern frontier models go *way* past Chinchilla — LLaMA-3 8B was trained on 15T tokens, that's ~1900 tokens per parameter. The reasoning: Chinchilla is compute-optimal for training, but inference cost per quality is better at over-trained models. Train longer, deploy a smaller model with better quality.

### Key training tricks

- **AdamW** with cosine LR schedule, linear warmup over 500-2000 steps.
- **Mixed precision**: BF16 for forward and backward; FP32 for optimizer state.
- **Gradient clipping** at norm 1.0 — prevents loss spikes from bad batches.
- **Z-loss**: small auxiliary loss penalizing logit magnitude, encourages logit stability.
- **ZeRO-3 / FSDP**: shard parameters, gradients, optimizer states across GPUs.
- **Activation checkpointing**: trade compute for memory by recomputing activations in backward pass.
- **Tensor parallelism**: split a single layer's matmul across multiple GPUs.
- **Pipeline parallelism**: place different layers on different GPUs.
- **Context parallelism**: split sequence dim across GPUs, for very long context training.

### Common mistakes / gotchas

1. **Confusing pretraining with fine-tuning loss**: Both use cross-entropy on next-token, but pretraining has no instruction mask — train on every token, including the prefix. SFT masks the instruction.
2. **Ignoring data quality**: 1T high-quality tokens beats 5T noisy tokens. Modern recipes spend more eng-effort on filtering than on training.
3. **Underestimating mixed precision pitfalls**: Pure FP16 can overflow/underflow; needs loss scaling. BF16 has the same exponent range as FP32 and trains stably without special tricks.

### Interview Q&A

**Q1. What's the pretraining loss?**
> "Cross-entropy on next-token prediction. For each position in a sequence, the model outputs a distribution over the vocabulary; we take the negative log of the probability assigned to the true next token; average over all positions and all sequences in the batch. That's the loss. The simplicity is the magic — this single objective, scaled to trillions of tokens, produces models that can reason, code, translate, summarize, and chat. No task-specific signals, no labels — just predict the next token."

**Q2. What are Chinchilla scaling laws?**
> "DeepMind's 2022 paper showed that for compute-optimal training, you want roughly 20 tokens per parameter. So a 70B model trained on 1.4T tokens is more compute-efficient than the same model on 300B tokens. Most pre-Chinchilla models were severely under-trained. The interesting twist is that modern frontier models like LLaMA-3 deliberately *over-train* — 200+ tokens per parameter — because inference cost per unit quality is better with a smaller, longer-trained model. Train more, deploy smaller."

**Q3. What is mixed precision training?**
> "We use FP16 or BF16 for forward and backward passes — half the memory, twice the throughput on tensor cores — but keep optimizer state and the master weights in FP32 to avoid precision loss in updates. BF16 has the same exponent range as FP32 (just lower mantissa precision), which makes it numerically stable without loss-scaling tricks. FP16 has narrower range and can underflow on small gradients, requiring dynamic loss scaling. Modern training uses BF16 by default."

**Q4. What is activation checkpointing?**
> "Trades compute for memory. Standard backprop caches every intermediate activation from the forward pass to use in the backward pass. For a 70B model, that's hundreds of GB. Activation checkpointing instead saves only a few activations — typically one per layer — and recomputes the rest during backward by re-running parts of the forward pass. About 30% more compute for 50-80% less memory. Essential for training large models without truly massive GPU clusters."

**Q5. How long does it take to pretrain a 70B model?**
> "Compute scales as 6 times parameters times tokens. For 70B on 15T tokens, that's about 6.3 × 10^24 FLOPs. At 1.5 PFLOPs sustained per H100, that's around 50,000 H100-days. On a cluster of 16,000 H100s, about 80 days end-to-end. Cost is roughly $10-20 million when you include data, salaries, and failed runs. This is why pretraining is restricted to a handful of labs — Anthropic, OpenAI, DeepMind, Meta, Mistral, DeepSeek, Qwen team, and a few others."

## 3.4 Stage 2 — Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)

After pretraining, the model is a brilliant text completer but a terrible assistant. Ask it "What's the capital of France?" and it might continue with another question instead of answering, because that's the pattern in the web data. SFT fixes this by training on (instruction, response) pairs in a chat format.

### The data

Examples look like:

```json
{
  "instruction": "Summarize the email below in one sentence.",
  "input": "Hi team, we need to move Monday's meeting to Tuesday at 3pm because the conference room is double-booked. Please RSVP. Thanks, Sarah",
  "output": "Sarah is moving Monday's meeting to Tuesday at 3pm due to a room conflict."
}
```

These pairs are written by humans, generated by stronger LLMs, or scraped from instruction-tuned datasets like FLAN, Dolly, OpenAssistant, ShareGPT, ultrafeedback.

### The training mechanics

Same loss as pretraining (cross-entropy on next-token), but **only over the response tokens** — we mask out the instruction. This focuses gradient signal on what the model is supposed to learn to produce, not what it's already good at consuming.

### Block diagram

```
   (instruction, response)
            │
            ▼
   ┌─────────────────────────┐
   │ Apply chat template     │   Wrap in role-tagged format like:
   │                         │   <|user|>Summarize this email...<|end|>
   │                         │   <|assistant|>Sarah is moving...<|end|>
   └────────┬────────────────┘
            │
            ▼
   ┌─────────────────────────┐
   │ Tokenize                │
   └────────┬────────────────┘
            │ token IDs
            ▼
   ┌─────────────────────────┐
   │ Build loss mask         │   1 for response tokens
   │                         │   0 for instruction tokens
   └────────┬────────────────┘
            │
            ▼
   ┌─────────────────────────┐
   │ Forward through model   │
   └────────┬────────────────┘
            │ logits
            ▼
   ┌─────────────────────────┐
   │ CE loss, masked by      │
   │ response-only mask      │
   └────────┬────────────────┘
            │
            ▼
       AdamW step
```

### Scale

- 100K to 10M examples typical. Most production fine-tunes are 10K-1M.
- The LIMA paper (2023) showed: 1K *carefully curated* high-quality examples beats 50K messy ones. Quality beats quantity by a wide margin.
- Hardest part is data curation, not training.

### Chat templates — the foot-gun

Every model family has a specific chat format. Mismatches break performance.

**LLaMA-3 chat template**:
```
<|begin_of_text|><|start_header_id|>system<|end_header_id|>

You are a helpful assistant.<|eot_id|><|start_header_id|>user<|end_header_id|>

Hi!<|eot_id|><|start_header_id|>assistant<|end_header_id|>

Hello! How can I help?<|eot_id|>
```

**ChatML (OpenAI, Qwen)**:
```
<|im_start|>system
You are a helpful assistant.<|im_end|>
<|im_start|>user
Hi!<|im_end|>
<|im_start|>assistant
Hello! How can I help?<|im_end|>
```

**Mistral**:
```
[INST] Hi! [/INST] Hello! How can I help?
```

The rule: **always use `tokenizer.apply_chat_template()`** from the model's official tokenizer. Don't hand-build templates. A subtle whitespace or special-token mismatch can drop accuracy 10-30%.

### LoRA / QLoRA — how teams actually fine-tune

Full SFT updates all 7B-70B parameters. LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) freezes the base model and inserts small low-rank matrices into attention layers — typically rank 8-64. Only those low-rank matrices train, reducing trainable params from 70B to ~50M-200M.

QLoRA (2023): same idea, but the frozen base is loaded in 4-bit quantization. Lets you fine-tune a 70B model on a single 80 GB GPU.

```
Standard fine-tune:        LoRA:
                          
  W (frozen)               W (frozen, often 4-bit)
                              +
                           A · B (rank r, trainable)
                              ↓
                           Effective W' = W + AB
```

For most production fine-tunes — including the kind we'd do at TrueBalance to specialize Claude-like behavior — LoRA is the default. Cheap, fast, almost-as-good.

### Common mistakes / gotchas

1. **Wrong chat template**: 10-30% accuracy hit. Use `apply_chat_template`.
2. **Catastrophic forgetting**: SFT on narrow data makes the model forget general capabilities. Always mix in 10-20% diverse instruction data.
3. **Too-high LR**: Standard pretraining LR (1e-4) is way too high for SFT. Use 1e-5 to 5e-5 for full FT, 2e-4 for LoRA.
4. **Loss mask off-by-one**: If you mask the wrong position by one, you train on instruction tokens — often manifests as the model regurgitating prompts.

### Resume tie-in

> At ResMed I built a clinical RAG chatbot, and we considered fine-tuning the base LLM on medical conversation pairs. We started with 5K curated medical dialogues and used QLoRA on a 13B base — trained on a single A100 in about 4 hours. The lift was real but the LIMA-style insight held: quality beat quantity. We later tried 50K examples, got worse results because of inconsistent labeling. The lesson stuck: invest in 1K great examples before reaching for 100K mediocre ones.

### Interview Q&A

**Q1. What does SFT do that pretraining doesn't?**
> "Pretraining gives you a brilliant text-completer that doesn't know how to answer questions or follow instructions — it just continues whatever pattern it sees. SFT fine-tunes the base model on curated (instruction, response) pairs in a chat format, with the loss masked so we only train on response tokens. After SFT, the model knows to respond when prompted instead of continuing the prompt as if it were a document. It also learns the chat format itself — turn boundaries, system messages, refusals."

**Q2. Why does LIMA's '1K is enough' result matter?**
> "It showed that quality dominates quantity in SFT. The LIMA paper fine-tuned LLaMA-65B on just 1,000 hand-crafted examples and got results competitive with models tuned on 50K-100K messy examples. The takeaway: invest engineering effort in curating a small set of consistently high-quality, diverse, carefully-formatted examples rather than scaling to 100K mediocre ones. This shaped how I now approach fine-tuning — start small with high quality, expand only if you can verify the new examples maintain that bar."

**Q3. LoRA vs full fine-tuning?**
> "LoRA freezes the base model and inserts small low-rank matrices A times B into each attention layer. Only those low-rank weights train — typically rank 8 to 64, reducing trainable parameters from billions to tens of millions. Quality is usually within 1-2 perplexity points of full FT. Memory and time are 10-100x lower. QLoRA goes further by loading the frozen base in 4-bit. So you can fine-tune a 70B model on a single 80GB GPU. For 90% of production fine-tunes, LoRA or QLoRA is the right call."

**Q4. Why does chat-template mismatch break things?**
> "The model was trained with a specific format — special tokens like `<|im_start|>` or `[INST]` mark role boundaries. If you send a prompt without those tokens, the model doesn't recognize where the user message ends and the assistant should begin, so generation gets confused. Even small changes — adding an extra space after `[INST]`, missing a `<|eot_id|>` — can drop accuracy 10-30%. Always use the model's official `apply_chat_template()` method; never hand-build templates."

**Q5. [Gotcha] You ran SFT on 1M examples but eval got worse. What happened?**
> "Most likely culprits in priority order. One, data quality — bad responses poison the model. Two, catastrophic forgetting — narrow fine-tuning erases general capability; mix in 10-20% general instruction data. Three, template mismatch — verify you're using the exact chat template the base was trained with. Four, learning rate too high — full FT wants 1e-5 to 5e-5; LoRA wants 2e-4. Five, eval mismatch — if your eval set is on a different distribution than training, you'd see this even with perfect training."

## 3.5 Stage 3 — Alignment (RLHF, DPO, and friends)

SFT gets you a model that follows instructions in correct format. Alignment gets you a model that produces responses humans actually *prefer*. The gap matters: a SFT-only model might give technically-correct but unhelpful responses, or technically-correct but unsafe ones.

### Why alignment exists

Two motivations.

First, **preference is finer-grained than format**. Two responses can both be technically correct, follow instructions, and be in the right format — but one is concise and warm while the other is verbose and clinical. Humans prefer one over the other, and SFT doesn't easily capture that signal. Alignment uses paired preference data — "which of these two responses is better?" — to dial in those subtleties.

Second, **safety**. SFT teaches the model to follow instructions well, including harmful ones if they're in training data. Alignment specifically trains the model to refuse certain categories — illegal advice, weapon synthesis, self-harm encouragement — while remaining helpful elsewhere. Constitutional AI and RLHF both build this in.

### 3.5.1 RLHF — the original recipe (OpenAI InstructGPT, 2022)

```
   ┌─────────────────────┐
   │  SFT model          │
   └──────┬──────────────┘
          │
          ▼
   For each prompt, generate N candidate responses
          │
          ▼
   ┌─────────────────────┐
   │  Human labelers     │  pick winner among pairs
   │  rank pairs         │
   └──────┬──────────────┘
          │
          ▼
   Preference dataset: (prompt, chosen, rejected)
          │
          ▼
   ┌─────────────────────┐
   │  Train Reward Model │  classifier: chosen > rejected
   │  (LM with scalar    │
   │   head)             │
   └──────┬──────────────┘
          │
          ▼
   ┌─────────────────────┐
   │  PPO loop:          │
   │  - Sample from SFT  │
   │  - Score with RM    │
   │  - Update policy    │
   │  - KL penalty vs    │
   │    SFT reference    │
   └──────┬──────────────┘
          │
          ▼
    Aligned model
```

Three components. (1) A reward model — a transformer with a scalar output head — trained on preference pairs to score "chosen > rejected." (2) PPO (Proximal Policy Optimization) — a reinforcement learning algorithm that updates the SFT model to maximize reward-model score. (3) A KL penalty against the SFT reference — prevents the policy from drifting too far and producing reward-hacked but bizarre outputs.

The PPO objective:
```
maximize  E[ R(prompt, response) ]  -  β · KL(π || π_ref)
```

Where R is the reward model, π is the current policy (being trained), π_ref is the SFT reference (frozen), β is the KL strength.

PPO is finicky. You're doing reinforcement learning on a 70B model. Variance is high. Reward hacking is real (the policy finds outputs the RM scores high but humans don't actually like). Engineering effort is substantial.

### 3.5.2 DPO — the modern default (2023)

Direct Preference Optimization, by Rafailov et al. The mathematical insight: you can derive a closed-form relationship between the optimal policy and the reward model under KL regularization, and use it to optimize the policy *directly* on preference data without ever training a reward model or running RL.

The DPO loss:
```
L_DPO = - log σ( β · log[π(y_w|x)/π_ref(y_w|x)] - β · log[π(y_l|x)/π_ref(y_l|x)] )
```

Plain English: take the log-ratio of the current policy's probability of the chosen response over the reference policy's probability. Do the same for the rejected response. Subtract. Apply sigmoid log. Minimize. The model learns to push up `chosen` probability relative to `rejected`, while the KL constraint comes for free from the log-ratio formulation.

### Why DPO won

- **Simpler**: One classification-style loss. No reward model. No PPO.
- **Stable**: Just gradient descent on a log-likelihood. None of PPO's instability.
- **Cheaper**: Single GPU, hours instead of days. No RL infrastructure.
- **Competitive quality**: Empirically matches or beats RLHF in most published comparisons.

Production default in 2024-2026. Anthropic uses DPO variants. Mistral, Qwen, LLaMA-3 alignment all use DPO or its derivatives.

### 3.5.3 Other variants worth knowing

| Method | One-line summary |
|--------|------------------|
| **RLAIF** | RLHF with an LLM judge instead of humans — feedback at scale. |
| **Constitutional AI** | Anthropic's recipe — LLM critiques itself based on a constitution; trains via SFT (critique-revise) and RL on AI-generated preferences. |
| **KTO** (Kahneman-Tversky) | Binary "good/bad" labels instead of pairs; cheaper labeling. |
| **ORPO** | Combines SFT + DPO in one stage. |
| **SimPO** | DPO without the reference model — saves compute and storage. |
| **IPO** (Identity PO) | Fixes DPO's tendency to over-optimize. |
| **NLHF** | Nash Learning from Human Feedback — game-theoretic alternative. |

### KL regularization — why it matters

Without the KL penalty against the SFT reference, alignment training collapses in three predictable ways:

- **Reward hacking**: The model finds adversarial outputs that score high on the RM but are bizarre to humans. "rrrrrrrr" or "the the the" might fool a poorly-trained RM.
- **Mode collapse**: All responses converge to a few generic formats — every answer starts "Certainly! I'd be happy to help. Here's..."
- **Capability loss**: General knowledge degrades because the model only optimizes for preference.

Too-high β (KL weight): policy can't move enough to actually improve. Too-low β: hacking. Sweet spot 0.1-0.5.

### Common mistakes / gotchas

1. **Treating RLHF as black-box magic**: It's just supervised learning of a reward model + RL with KL constraints. Understand each piece.
2. **Skipping the SFT reference in DPO**: The reference policy is what anchors the DPO loss. Without it, the math breaks.
3. **Insufficient diversity in preference data**: If labelers all have the same blind spot, the RM/policy learns it.

### Interview Q&A

**Q1. Why DPO instead of RLHF?**
> "DPO derives a closed-form relationship between the optimal policy under KL-regularized RLHF and the preference data, letting us optimize the policy directly on (chosen, rejected) pairs without training a reward model or running PPO. Result: one classification-style loss, no RL, more stable, runs on a single GPU in hours, matches or beats RLHF empirically. Production default in 2025-2026 — Anthropic's variants, LLaMA-3, Mistral, Qwen all use DPO or its derivatives. The original RLHF pipeline still has its place for very large-scale online learning, but DPO is the right starting point for almost every production alignment job."

**Q2. What does the KL penalty do in alignment?**
> "Anchors the policy to the SFT reference. Without it, the model finds adversarial outputs that score high on the reward model but are bizarre to humans — that's reward hacking. The KL term penalizes deviation from the SFT distribution, so the policy can only move within a 'corridor' around the reference. Beta is the strength: too high and the policy can't move enough; too low and it hacks. Typical values 0.1 to 0.5. In DPO the KL constraint is implicit in the log-ratio formulation, which is part of why it's more stable."

**Q3. Walk me through the full RLHF pipeline.**
> "Start with the SFT model. For each prompt in a held-out set, generate N candidate responses, typically 4. Human labelers rank pairs — chosen vs rejected. Train a reward model on these pairs — a transformer with a scalar output head, trained with a pairwise ranking loss to score chosen higher than rejected. Then run PPO: sample responses from the current policy, score them with the RM, compute the policy gradient with a KL penalty against the SFT reference, update the policy. Repeat for thousands of iterations. The result is the aligned model."

**Q4. What is Constitutional AI?**
> "Anthropic's alignment recipe, 2022. Instead of human feedback, you encode a 'constitution' — a list of principles the model should follow. The model generates a response, then critiques its own response against the constitution, then revises. This generates training data of (response, critique, revised-response) triples used for SFT. Then you do RL where the reward is generated by an LLM scoring outputs against the constitution. CAI scales preference labeling beyond what humans can do alone, and produces models with more transparent and steerable refusal behavior. Anthropic's models — including Claude — use a CAI-derived recipe."

**Q5. [Gotcha] After DPO, your model hallucinates more. Why?**
> "Preference-optimization targets 'what humans prefer,' which is a proxy for 'what's true.' Humans often prefer confident-sounding answers, longer responses, or politely-formatted answers — even when those are wrong. The reward signal can train the model to be more confidently wrong. Mitigations: factuality-aware reward models that include a 'is this true?' signal, RAG grounding to anchor responses in retrieved sources, lower beta to reduce drift, and explicit hallucination eval in your test set so you catch this before deployment."

**Q6. What's RLAIF and when do you use it?**
> "RL from AI Feedback. Same structure as RLHF but the labeler is an LLM, not a human. You ask a strong LLM (often the same family or a stronger one) to choose the better of two candidate responses given some criteria. This scales beyond what humans can label and is cheaper. It works well when the criteria are crisp — code correctness, schema validity, instruction-following — but worse for subtle quality dimensions where the AI labeler shares the policy's blind spots. Constitutional AI is a specific RLAIF recipe; broadly, RLAIF is the category."

## 3.6 Stage 4 — Inference

Now we're in your day-to-day. The model is trained; we need to serve it efficiently to thousands of users.

### The decoding loop

```
input = tokenize(prompt)
kv_cache = empty

# PREFILL — process the prompt in parallel
logits = model.forward(input, kv_cache=kv_cache)
                               # populates kv_cache for all prompt tokens
next_token = sample(logits[-1])

while not stop_condition:
    # DECODE — one token at a time
    logits = model.forward(next_token, kv_cache=kv_cache)
                                  # only new token in; attends to cache
    next_token = sample(logits[0])
    output.append(next_token)

return detokenize(output)
```

Two phases with very different performance characteristics.

### Prefill — compute-bound

Process all prompt tokens in parallel — say 1,000 prompt tokens in one forward pass per layer. That's a giant matmul that saturates tensor cores. The GPU runs near peak utilization. Wall time scales with prompt length × model size, but per-token throughput is high.

### Decode — memory-bandwidth-bound

Sequential — one token at a time. Each forward pass is a thin matmul (just one token wide). It under-utilizes compute. The bottleneck is streaming model weights from HBM to compute units. For a 70B BF16 model, that's 140 GB streamed per token. At 3 TB/s HBM bandwidth, that's ~50 ms minimum per token, even on H100.

### Latency metrics — what to measure

| Metric | What it measures | Target |
|--------|------------------|--------|
| **TTFT** (Time to First Token) | prefill latency + first-token compute | < 500 ms for chat |
| **TPOT** (Time Per Output Token) | steady-state decode speed | < 50 ms for chat |
| **ITL** (Inter-Token Latency) | same as TPOT | streaming UX |
| **E2E** | total time for full response | depends on output length |

The user experience depends on both. Long TTFT feels like the system froze. Slow TPOT feels like watching a teletype. Good UX needs both under target.

### Continuous batching — vLLM's secret sauce

```
NAIVE STATIC BATCHING:
    Request A (long)   ████████████████ done at t=8s
    Request B (short)  ██               done at t=2s, but waits...
    Request C (medium) ████████         done at t=6s, waits...
    
    All three wait for the slowest. Throughput dies on heterogeneous loads.

CONTINUOUS BATCHING:
    Request A          ████████████████ keeps going
    Request B          ██  | (Request D admitted) ████████
    Request C          ████████ | (Request E admitted) ████████
    
    Finished requests are evicted every step; new ones admitted.
    GPU is always full of in-flight tokens.
```

5-10x throughput gain on heterogeneous workloads. This single optimization is why vLLM dominated the open-source serving space.

### PagedAttention — KV-cache as virtual memory

KV-cache for one request is stored as a contiguous tensor in naive implementations. But requests have different and changing sizes. Internal fragmentation wastes memory.

PagedAttention treats KV-cache like OS virtual memory:
- Fixed-size blocks (typically 16 tokens worth of K, V each).
- Each request gets a "page table" mapping its logical positions to physical blocks.
- Blocks are allocated and freed on demand; no fragmentation.
- Bonus: prefix caching — if two requests share a prompt prefix, they share the same physical blocks.

Result: 2-4x more concurrent requests on the same GPU vs naive implementations.

### Speculative decoding — getting faster decode

The slowest part of decode is the sequential single-token generation. Speculative decoding parallelizes this:

```
   Big model       Draft model (small)
        │                  │
        │                  ▼
        │         Generate k candidate tokens
        │         sequentially: t1, t2, ..., tk
        │                  │
        │                  ▼
        │         Pass [t1...tk] to big model
        │                  │
        ▼                  │
   Verify all k in ◀───────┘
   one forward pass
        │
        ▼
   Accept longest valid prefix
   (where draft and big model
    argmax agree)
```

Big model's forward pass is bandwidth-bound regardless of how many tokens it processes — verifying k tokens costs roughly the same as verifying 1. So we get 2-3x speedup, with mathematically identical output distribution.

### Prefix caching

Many production workloads share common prefixes — system prompts, RAG contexts, few-shot examples. Hash the prefix; reuse the KV-cache.

Example: an agent with a 2K-token system prompt. Without prefix caching, every request re-prefills those 2K tokens. With caching, the second request skips prefill on the prefix entirely. TTFT drops 5-10x.

### Chunked prefill

Long prompts (32K context RAG) take seconds to prefill, blocking the GPU and starving short requests. Chunked prefill splits the long prefill into smaller chunks that interleave with in-flight decode steps, smoothing tail latency.

### Decoding strategies — picking how to sample

| Strategy | What it does | When to use |
|----------|-------------|-------------|
| **Greedy** | argmax at each step | Math, code, when determinism matters |
| **Beam search** | maintain top-k candidate sequences | Translation, summarization (low diversity OK) |
| **Temperature** | scale logits by 1/T before softmax | Tune randomness — T=0 is greedy, T=1 is "natural" |
| **Top-k** | sample only from top-k tokens | Cap rare-token noise |
| **Top-p (nucleus)** | sample from smallest set with cumulative p ≥ threshold | Adapts to distribution shape — production default |
| **Min-p** | exclude tokens below p × max_prob | Newer, more robust than top-p |

### Side-by-side example outputs

Prompt: "The story begins on a stormy night..."

- **Greedy**: "The story begins on a stormy night and the wind was howling and the rain was pouring and the wind was howling..."
- **T=0.7, top-p=0.9**: "The story begins on a stormy night, the kind that makes window-frames shudder. Margaret pulled her coat tighter as she crossed the moor."
- **T=1.5, no top-p**: "The story begins on a stormy night zenith forensic spinach umbrella narrative collapses..."

Greedy is deterministic but repetitive (gets stuck in loops). Moderate temperature with top-p gives natural variation. Too-high temperature collapses to noise.

### KV-cache size formula — memorize this

```
KV cache (bytes) = 2 (K and V) × n_layers × n_kv_heads × d_k × seq_len × dtype_bytes
```

LLaMA-3 70B at 8K context with GQA (8 KV heads):
```
2 × 80 × 8 × 128 × 8192 × 2 = 2.7 GB per request
```

Memory budget on 80 GB H100:
- 70B FP16 weights: 140 GB → doesn't fit on one H100, need TP=2 or quantize.
- FP8 weights: 70 GB → fits with ~10 GB for cache → 3-4 concurrent users at 8K.
- INT4 weights: 35 GB → fits with 45 GB for cache → 16+ concurrent users.

This is why quantization matters in production.

### Common mistakes / gotchas

1. **Confusing TTFT and TPOT**: They have separate optimization paths. Long TTFT = improve prefill (more compute). Slow TPOT = improve decode (more bandwidth, max-batch).
2. **Ignoring chunked prefill in mixed workloads**: Without it, one long-context request blocks the GPU and tanks p99 latency for short requests.
3. **Using temperature=0 expecting determinism**: Floating-point non-associativity means GPU reductions can give last-bit-different results, which can flip argmax ties. True determinism needs deterministic kernels and fixed batch order.
4. **Forgetting to set stop tokens**: Without explicit stop tokens, the model generates until max_tokens — wasting compute and potentially producing garbage after the real answer.

We measured TTFT and TPOT separately — TTFT was dominated by Claude's prefill time over our 8K-token system prompt + retrieved context, and we cut it 6x by getting prefix caching working with Anthropic's prompt-caching API. TPOT was Claude's natural rate. The mental model of "prefill is compute-bound, decode is memory-bandwidth-bound" maps cleanly to closed-API inference too — caching the prefix cuts the compute-bound step.

### Interview Q&A

**Q1. Why is decode memory-bandwidth-bound?**
> "Each decode step processes a single new token but has to stream the entire model weights and the entire KV-cache from HBM to compute units. For a 70B BF16 model that's 140 GB of weights plus several GB of cache, every single token. Compute per token is tiny — a few hundred GFLOPs against 2-3 TB/s of memory bandwidth. So wall time per token is dominated by `weights_size / bandwidth`. The only way to amortize this is max-batching — serve N concurrent requests and you stream weights once for N tokens. That's why vLLM's continuous batching is so impactful."

**Q2. What's the prefill-decode distinction?**
> "Prefill processes all prompt tokens in parallel in one forward pass per layer — a giant matmul that saturates tensor cores, compute-bound. Decode generates one token at a time — sequential, each forward pass is a thin matmul that under-utilizes compute and is bottlenecked by streaming weights from HBM. Different optimization levers: prefill benefits from tensor parallelism, large batch, FlashAttention; decode benefits from max-batching, paged attention, speculative decoding, KV-cache quantization."

**Q3. Explain continuous batching.**
> "Naive static batching waits for the slowest request in a batch to complete before the GPU moves on. With heterogeneous request lengths — short chat plus long RAG plus tiny completion — the short requests sit idle waiting. Continuous batching swaps finished requests out *every decode step* and admits new ones. The GPU is always full of in-flight tokens. Result is 5-10x throughput gain on heterogeneous workloads, which is the dominant production case. vLLM was the first to popularize this, and it's now standard in TGI, TensorRT-LLM, and others."

**Q4. What is PagedAttention?**
> "vLLM's KV-cache management. Naive implementations store cache as a contiguous tensor per request, which fragments memory as requests come and go with different lengths. PagedAttention treats cache like OS virtual memory — fixed-size blocks (typically 16 tokens of K and V each), allocated on demand from a free pool, with each request having a page table mapping logical positions to physical blocks. No fragmentation, plus you get prefix sharing for free — two requests with the same prompt share blocks. Net result: 2-4x more concurrent requests vs naive."

**Q5. Speculative decoding — how does it work?**
> "Use a small fast 'draft' model to generate k candidate tokens sequentially. Pass all k to the big main model in one forward pass for verification. Since the big model's forward pass is bandwidth-bound, verifying k tokens costs roughly the same as verifying 1. Accept the longest prefix where draft and main agree on argmax; if they disagree at position j, accept tokens 0 to j-1 and use the main model's argmax at j. Net 2-3x speedup with mathematically identical output distribution. Variants include Medusa, EAGLE, self-speculative."

**Q6. TTFT vs TPOT — which matters more?**
> "Depends on use case. For chat UX, TTFT matters most — users need to see the first word fast or it feels frozen. Good target is under 500 ms. For batch summarization or analytics, end-to-end latency matters more than streaming feel. For agent loops where the LLM responds many times in sequence, TPOT dominates total latency. Most production deployments target both: TTFT under 500 ms, TPOT under 50 ms. Different optimization levers for each — prefix caching and chunked prefill for TTFT; quantization, max batching, speculative decoding for TPOT."

**Q7. Walk me through decoding strategies.**
> "Greedy picks the argmax at each step — deterministic but often gets stuck in repetitive loops. Beam search keeps top-k candidate sequences and explores in parallel — good for translation but reduces diversity. Temperature scales logits by 1 over T before softmax — T=0 is greedy, T=1 is natural, higher is more random. Top-k samples from the top-k logits — caps rare-token noise but is rigid. Top-p, nucleus, samples from the smallest set whose cumulative probability exceeds p — adapts to distribution shape, peakier at confident tokens, broader at uncertain ones. Production default is temperature 0.7 to 1.0 with top-p 0.9 to 0.95. For code or math, temperature 0 with greedy."

**Q8. What is prefix caching, when is it worth it?**
> "If many requests share a common prompt prefix — system prompts, RAG context, few-shot examples — you can cache the KV-cache for that prefix and reuse across requests, skipping prefill on the cached portion entirely. Anthropic's API supports this explicitly with `cache_control` markers; vLLM does it automatically based on prefix hash. Worth it when prefix is large (> 1K tokens) and reused frequently. TTFT drops 5-10x in agent workloads. Cost: cache eviction policy must be tuned — too aggressive and you re-prefill; too lax and you fill memory with cold caches."

**Q9. [Gotcha] Temperature=0 in vLLM sometimes gives different outputs. Why?**
> "Floating-point non-associativity in GPU reductions. Atomic adds and tensor-core fused-multiply-adds can produce last-bit-different results depending on the order operands are summed, which depends on batch composition and other concurrent requests. With identical inputs and identical seed, you can still get different last-bit logits, and softmax argmax can flip on ties. True determinism needs deterministic CUDA kernels (often via `torch.use_deterministic_algorithms(True)`), fixed batch order, and disabling some optimizations. In production, you trade strict determinism for throughput."

**Q10. KV-cache quantization — does it matter?**
> "Yes, a lot. Standard KV-cache is BF16 (2 bytes per element). Quantizing to FP8 saves 2x memory with essentially zero quality loss. INT8 saves another bit but introduces a small quality degradation in long-context scenarios. INT4 has noticeable quality loss but lets you fit huge contexts on small GPUs. Production: FP8 KV-cache via `--kv-cache-dtype fp8` in vLLM is the default, doubling concurrent users at no quality cost."

## 3.7 Tool-calling / function-calling

Modern chat LLMs can emit structured JSON to call external tools — the foundation of agents.

### The flow

```
User: What's the weather in Abu Dhabi?
   │
   ▼
System prompt includes tool schemas:
   tools = [
     {
       "name": "get_weather",
       "parameters": {"city": "string"},
       "description": "Get current weather"
     }
   ]
   │
   ▼
Model emits: {"tool": "get_weather", "args": {"city": "Abu Dhabi"}}
   │
   ▼
Framework (Anthropic SDK, LangChain) parses, dispatches:
   tool_result = get_weather(city="Abu Dhabi")  →  {"temp_c": 34, "humidity": 65}
   │
   ▼
Inject tool result back as a "tool" role turn
   │
   ▼
Model generates: "It's 34°C and 65% humidity in Abu Dhabi."
   │
   ▼
User sees clean answer.
```

### Under the hood

- Model is SFT+DPO'd on tool-use traces in the model family's specific tool-call format.
- Constrained decoding (Outlines, xgrammar, JSON mode) can enforce schema validity at generation time.
- Framework handles dispatch, error recovery, multi-turn tool use, max-turn caps.

### Resume tie-in

> The Claude-powered ML workspace at TrueBalance was pure tool-calling architecture. Claude had access to Jira, GitHub, Athena, Jenkins, and our internal feature store. Each request became a multi-turn conversation: "Find recent failed Jenkins builds for project X, summarize root causes, file Jira tickets." Claude orchestrated the tool calls; my code dispatched them and injected results. The hardest part wasn't the LLM — it was idempotency, error handling, max-turn caps to prevent infinite loops, and structured output validation. Tool-calling looks easy in the demo and hard in production.

### Interview Q&A

**Q1. How does tool-calling work under the hood?**
> "The model is SFT'd and aligned on traces of tool use in a specific format — usually a JSON-like structure or special tokens. The system prompt describes available tools' JSON schemas. At inference, when the model decides to call a tool, it generates a JSON object with the tool name and arguments. Constrained decoding can enforce schema validity. The framework parses the JSON, dispatches the actual tool call, captures the result, and injects it back as a 'tool' role turn. The model continues from there — possibly calling more tools or producing the final answer. Loop continues until the model emits no more tool calls or hits a max-turn limit."

**Q2. What's constrained decoding?**
> "Decoding where the model is restricted to producing tokens that conform to a grammar or schema. At each step, you compute the model's logits, then apply a mask that zeroes out tokens incompatible with the grammar — e.g., after `{"name":` you can only produce a quoted string. Libraries: Outlines, xgrammar, lm-format-enforcer, JSON mode in many APIs. Prevents the model from emitting invalid JSON or outputs that fail downstream parsing. Worth it for any tool-calling or structured-output pipeline."

## 3.9 Comprehensive Q&A — LLMs end-to-end

**Q1. Walk through the full LLM pipeline.**
> "Pretraining on trillions of tokens with next-token cross-entropy gives a base model with broad knowledge. SFT on curated instruction-response pairs in chat format teaches it to follow instructions. Alignment — DPO is the modern default, RLHF the historical version — tunes for human preference using paired-choice data with KL constraint. Deployment uses vLLM or TensorRT-LLM with FP8 quantization, paged attention, continuous batching, prefix caching, and monitoring. Each stage has a different cost profile — pretraining millions, SFT and alignment thousands, deployment per-token operational."

**Q2. Why DPO over RLHF?**
> "DPO derives a closed-form relationship between the optimal RLHF policy and the preference data, letting you optimize directly on (chosen, rejected) pairs without training a reward model or running PPO. One classification-style loss, no RL, more stable, runs on a single GPU in hours. Empirically matches or beats RLHF. Production default. RLHF still has value for very large-scale online learning but DPO is the right starting point for almost every alignment job today."

**Q3. What is KL divergence's role in RLHF?**
> "It anchors the policy to the SFT reference. Without it, you get reward hacking — outputs that score high on the RM but are bizarre to humans — and mode collapse. The KL penalty restricts the policy to a corridor around SFT. Beta is the strength: too high and the policy can't move; too low and it hacks. In DPO the KL constraint is implicit in the log-ratio formulation, which is part of why DPO is more stable."

**Q4. BPE vs SentencePiece vs WordPiece?**
> "BPE merges most-frequent pairs greedily — used in GPT-2 character-level, GPT-3/4 byte-level, LLaMA-3. WordPiece merges by likelihood — used in BERT. SentencePiece is a framework that operates on raw Unicode without language-specific pre-tokenization, using either BPE or Unigram underneath — used in T5, mT5, Gemma, LLaMA-2, Mistral. Byte-level BPE has the property of zero OOV ever, since every UTF-8 byte is representable."

**Q5. Why byte-level BPE?**
> "Three reasons. One, no OOV — every byte is representable so any text encodes. Two, multilingual — handles all scripts uniformly without language-specific pre-tokenizers. Three, robust to weird inputs — emoji, code, hashtags, markdown, all tokenize consistently. Modern frontier models — GPT-4 via tiktoken, LLaMA-3, Claude — all use byte-level BPE."

**Q6. What is exposure bias in autoregressive training?**
> "Training uses teacher forcing — the model always sees the ground-truth prefix when predicting the next token. At inference, it sees its own previous predictions, which can be wrong. Errors compound as generation continues. Mitigations include scheduled sampling during training (occasionally use the model's own prediction as the next prefix), contrastive decoding at inference, and various sampling tweaks. In modern LLMs the issue is somewhat mitigated by massive scale — the model is so good at recovering from minor errors — but it's still real for long generations."

**Q7. Decoding strategies — pick one and explain.**
> "Top-p, nucleus sampling. Sample from the smallest set of tokens whose cumulative probability exceeds p. So at p=0.9, if the top token has probability 0.95, you sample only from that one token. If the top three tokens have probabilities 0.4, 0.3, 0.2 (cumulative 0.9), you sample from all three. Adapts to distribution shape — peaky when confident, broad when uncertain. More robust than top-k, which always picks k regardless of how peaked the distribution is. Production default for chat with temperature 0.7 to 1.0."

**Q8. Speculative decoding — why does it work?**
> "Two facts. One, the big model's forward pass is bandwidth-bound, so verifying k tokens at once costs only slightly more than verifying one. Two, a small draft model can generate plausible candidates much faster sequentially than the big model would. Combine: draft model generates k candidates sequentially in a few ms; big model verifies all k in one forward pass; accept the longest valid prefix. End result is 2-3x speedup with the big model's exact output distribution preserved. Variants like Medusa add multiple decode heads to the big model itself; EAGLE uses a layer-skipped version of the model."

**Q9. [Gotcha] You ran SFT on 1M examples but eval got worse. Why?**
> "Most likely: data quality — bad examples poison the model. Or catastrophic forgetting — narrow data erases general capability; mix in 10-20% diverse instruction data. Or template mismatch — exact special tokens matter. Or LR too high — full FT wants 1e-5 to 5e-5; LoRA wants 2e-4. Or eval distribution shift — you trained on one distribution and eval on another."

**Q10. [Gotcha] Your SFT model is great but it hallucinates more after DPO/RLHF. Why?**
> "Preference optimization targets human preference, which is a proxy for truth. Humans often prefer confident-sounding answers, longer responses, polite refusals — even when the content is wrong. The reward model and the policy can both learn to be confidently wrong because that scores higher on preference. Mitigations: factuality-aware reward models that include a 'is this correct?' signal, RAG grounding so responses cite sources, lower KL beta, and explicit hallucination evaluation in your test set."

**Q11. What is continuous batching?**
> "Naive batching waits for all requests in a batch to finish before the next batch starts. With variable-length workloads, the slowest request blocks everyone. Continuous batching swaps finished sequences out and admits new ones every decoding step, keeping the GPU always full of in-flight tokens. 5-10x throughput gain over static batching on heterogeneous workloads. vLLM's core innovation, now standard in TGI and TensorRT-LLM."

**Q12. What is PagedAttention?**
> "vLLM's KV-cache memory management. Treats cache as fixed-size pages (typically 16 tokens of K and V each), allocated from a free pool, with each request having a page table. No fragmentation as requests come and go with different lengths. Bonus: prefix caching — two requests with the same prompt share physical pages. 2-4x more concurrent requests vs naive contiguous-cache implementations."

**Q13. Prefill-decode distinction?**
> "Prefill processes the prompt in parallel — compute-bound, near peak GPU utilization. Decode generates one token at a time — memory-bandwidth-bound, has to stream weights and KV-cache per step. Different optimizations: prefill benefits from tensor parallelism and FlashAttention; decode benefits from max-batching, paged attention, speculative decoding, KV-cache quantization. Mixed workloads need chunked prefill to interleave."

**Q14. TTFT vs TPOT — which matters more?**
> "Use-case dependent. Chat UX: TTFT matters most — long TTFT feels frozen. Target under 500 ms. Streaming generation: TPOT dominates after first token. Target under 50 ms. Agent loops: TPOT matters more because many short responses. Batch jobs: end-to-end latency. Most production deployments target both. Different optimizations: prefix caching for TTFT, max-batching and speculative decoding for TPOT."

**Q15. Why is decode memory-bandwidth-bound?**
> "Each decode step processes one token but streams the entire model from HBM — 140 GB for a 70B BF16 model — plus KV-cache. Compute is tiny against 2-3 TB/s bandwidth. Wall time is dominated by `weights_size / bandwidth`. Max-batching is the only way to amortize the weight transfer across many concurrent users, multiplying throughput linearly with batch size up to compute saturation. That's why vLLM's continuous batching is so important."

**Q16. What is chunked prefill?**
> "Splits a long prefill into smaller chunks that interleave with in-flight decode steps. Long prompts (32K context RAG) take seconds to prefill, blocking the GPU and tanking p99 for short requests. Chunked prefill smooths this by allocating a few decode steps between prefill chunks, so latency stays bounded. vLLM 0.6+ supports it. Important for chat applications with long RAG context mixed with short interactive queries."

**Q17. Constitutional AI vs RLAIF?**
> "Constitutional AI (Anthropic, 2022) is a specific recipe: define a constitution of principles, have the model critique its responses against the constitution, train via SFT on critique-revise traces, then RL on AI-generated preferences. RLAIF is the broader category: RL from AI feedback, where any LLM acts as judge instead of humans. CAI is one instance of RLAIF. Anthropic's models — Claude — use a CAI-derived recipe."

**Q18. What is PPO's clip in RLHF?**
> "Clips the probability ratio `pi_new / pi_old` to `[1 - epsilon, 1 + epsilon]` (typically epsilon = 0.1 to 0.2). Prevents one update step from moving the policy so far that subsequent updates diverge. Without clipping, RLHF is extremely unstable — a single noisy gradient can blow up training. Clip is what makes PPO 'proximal' — close to the previous policy."

**Q19. Chain-of-Thought — decoding or training trick?**
> "Both. As prompting, 'Let's think step by step' or few-shot CoT examples elicit intermediate reasoning at inference time, which empirically improves accuracy on math, logic, multi-step tasks. As training, you fine-tune on CoT traces — examples where the response includes step-by-step reasoning — so the model reliably produces a reasoning trace before answers. OpenAI's o1 family is trained to do extensive CoT internally. Most modern aligned models like Claude have CoT-style reasoning baked in."

**Q20. Self-consistency vs Tree-of-Thoughts?**
> "Self-consistency samples N CoT traces with high temperature, then takes the majority vote on the final answer. Cheap and effective for math problems. Tree-of-Thoughts does explicit search — BFS or DFS — over branching reasoning states, with a value function evaluating intermediate states. More expensive but needed for harder planning tasks. Quality ordering: ToT > self-consistency > single CoT > direct answer. ToT is rare in production due to cost; self-consistency is common."

**Q21. [Gotcha] Temperature=0 in vLLM sometimes gives different outputs. Why?**
> "Floating-point non-determinism in GPU reductions. Atomic adds and fused tensor-core MAC ops can produce last-bit-different results depending on operand order, which depends on batch composition and concurrent requests. Identical inputs and identical seed can still produce slightly different logits, flipping argmax ties. True determinism needs deterministic kernels via `torch.use_deterministic_algorithms`, fixed batch order, and disabling some optimizations. Production trades strict determinism for throughput."

**Q22. How does tool-calling work under the hood?**
> "Model is SFT and aligned on tool-use traces in a specific format — JSON-like or special tokens. System prompt describes available tools' schemas. At inference, the model emits JSON for a tool call when appropriate. Constrained decoding can enforce schema validity. Framework parses, dispatches the actual tool, injects the result as a 'tool' role turn. Loop continues until model stops calling tools or hits a max-turn cap."

**Q23. What does the chat template do?**
> "Wraps user, assistant, system turns with model-specific control tokens — `<|eot_id|>` for LLaMA-3, `<|im_end|>` for ChatML, `[/INST]` for Mistral — so the model recognizes role boundaries. A subtle whitespace mismatch can drop accuracy 10-30%. Always use `tokenizer.apply_chat_template()` from the official tokenizer; never hand-build."

**Q24. Mixture of Experts (MoE) — quick explanation?**
> "Replace the dense FFN with N experts; a learned router picks K of them — usually K=2 — per token. Total params can be huge — Mixtral 8x7B has 47B total — but compute per token is just K times one expert, e.g., 14B. So you get the quality of a much larger model at the inference cost of a smaller one. Training is tricky — load balancing, router training, capacity factor. Mixtral, DeepSeek-V3, GPT-4 (rumored), and Claude (likely) all use MoE."

**Q25. KV-cache quantization?**
> "Quantize the KV-cache itself, not just weights, to FP8/INT8/INT4. FP8 is essentially lossless and saves 2x memory, doubling concurrent users. INT8 has small quality drop in long-context. INT4 has noticeable degradation. Configure in vLLM with `--kv-cache-dtype fp8`. Production default is FP8 for the doubling at no cost."
